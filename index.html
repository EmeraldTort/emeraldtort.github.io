🌿 Staple Weeds & Plants
▶
These should form the majority (80%+) of your Horsefield's diet. Wild weeds are ideal — free, nutritious, and natural.
  • Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale) — entire plant including flowers. Excellent calcium source.
  • Plantain (Plantago major / lanceolata) — leaves and seed heads.
  • Clover (Trifolium spp.) — leaves and flowers. High in calcium.
  • Sow thistle (Sonchus spp.) — very nutritious.
  • Hawkbit / Hawkweed — dandelion-like weeds, excellent.
  • Mallow (Malva spp.) — leaves and flowers.
  • Chicory (Cichorium intybus) — leaves.
  • Bindweed (Calystegia / Convolvulus) — leaves only.
  • Milk thistle — leaves (watch for spines with young tortoises).
  • Opuntia cactus pads — excellent, spine-free pads only.
🥬 Occasional Vegetables
▶
Use these sparingly — no more than 10–15% of total diet. High water content can cause loose stools.
  • Dark leafy greens: kale, spring greens, watercress, rocket
  • Endive / Escarole — good calcium:phosphorus ratio
  • Lambs lettuce — fine in small amounts
  • Red / green lettuce — small amounts only (mostly water)
  • Pumpkin / squash leaves — occasional
  • Edible flowers: rose petals, hibiscus, cornflower, marigold
⚠️ Foods to Avoid
▶
These can cause serious harm — some are toxic. Never feed these.
  • Fruit — Horsefields are from arid steppes and are not adapted to sugar. Fruit feeds gut bacteria that produce gas and can cause fatal bloating.
  • Spinach & beetroot leaves — high oxalic acid binds calcium → MBD
  • Iceberg lettuce — almost zero nutrition, causes diarrhoea
  • Brassicas in excess (cabbage, broccoli, Brussels) — goitrogenic, disrupt thyroid
  • Rhubarb — toxic
  • Avocado — toxic
  • Onion, garlic, leek — toxic
  • Mushrooms — not a natural food, avoid
  • Beans & legumes — too high in protein, causes kidney damage long-term
  • Any animal protein — Horsefields are herbivores
  • Dog/cat food — toxic, far too high in protein
  • Bread, pasta, rice — processed, harmful
  • Buttercup — toxic
  • Foxglove — highly toxic
  • Daffodil / Narcissus — toxic
  • Nightshade family (potato tops, tomato leaves) — toxic
💧 Calcium & Supplements
▶

Calcium is critical for shell and bone development.

  • Cuttlebone: leave a piece in the enclosure at all times. The tortoise will gnaw on it as needed.
  • Calcium powder: dust food lightly 2–3× per week (plain calcium carbonate, no D3 — UVB provides D3).
  • Limestone flour: can be sprinkled on food.
  • A varied weed-based diet usually provides enough calcium without heavy supplementation.
  • Avoid supplements with D3 if providing adequate UVB — vitamin D3 toxicity is possible.
📅 Feeding Schedule
▶
Adult tortoiseFeed 5–6 days per week. One rest day is fine.
Juvenile (<5 years)Feed daily — growing rapidly.
Portion sizeRoughly the size of the tortoise's shell in volume.
Time of dayMid-morning after basking to warm up digestion.
FastingDo not fast for more than 2 days unless pre-hibernation.
Never feed a cold tortoise. They must be warm enough (basking first) for digestion to work. Undigested food rots in the gut.

🚨 Seek Vet Immediately
▶
These are emergencies. Contact a reptile vet same day.
  • Runny nose / nasal discharge — could be Runny Nose Syndrome (RNS), a respiratory infection
  • Open-mouth breathing or wheezing — respiratory distress
  • Eyes swollen shut or sunken — dehydration or infection
  • Mouth gaping or foaming — possible stomatitis
  • Shell soft, crumbling, or pitting — Metabolic Bone Disease or shell rot
  • Pyramid-shaped scutes that are excessive — permanent but can indicate past/present husbandry issues
  • Not moving for several days (when not hibernating)
  • Prolapsed organ — keep moist, emergency
  • Sudden weight loss
  • Blood in urine or faeces
  • Limb swelling or lameness
  • Tortoise found upside down and very lethargic — possible injury
⚠️ Monitor Closely
▶
These warrant observation. Seek vet advice if they persist beyond a day or two.
  • Refusing food — 2–3 days in active season. Check temperatures first.
  • Spending all day buried / hiding — check temps; may be too cold
  • Loose or watery droppings — check diet for excess water-content veg or fruit
  • White chalky urates appearing orange or yellow — dehydration; increase baths
  • Scratching at eyes — check for substrate in eyes or infection
  • Uneven walking or head tilt
  • Lethargy outside hibernation
✅ Signs of Good Health
▶
A healthy Horsefield tortoise should:
  • Have bright, clear, alert eyes
  • Have a dry, clean nose with no discharge
  • Be active during the warmer part of the day
  • Have a hard, well-formed shell (slight pyramiding may be normal in captive-bred animals)
  • Pass solid, dark droppings with chalky white urates
  • Show interest in food
  • Bask regularly under the heat/UVB lamp
  • Gain weight steadily (weigh monthly)
